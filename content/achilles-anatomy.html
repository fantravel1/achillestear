The Muscles: Gastrocnemius & Soleus

The Achilles is the shared tendon of two distinct muscles â€” and understanding each is critical to effective prevention training.

Muscle Origin Key Function Dominant Activity
Gastrocnemius Posterior femur (above knee) Plantarflexion + knee flexion. Fast, explosive movements. Sprinting, jumping, sudden acceleration. High-speed, explosive sport.
Soleus Posterior tibia and fibula (below knee) Plantarflexion only. Postural, slow twitch, endurance-focused. Walking, running at moderate pace, prolonged standing. Controls forward tibial lean.

The soleus is the underdog of Achilles prevention. Most athletes train the gastrocnemius (standing calf raises) but neglect the soleus. The soleus accounts for ~60% of Achilles force during running. Target it with seated calf raises (knee bent 90Â°) and bent-knee single-leg calf work.

The Myotendinous Junction

Where the calf muscles transition into the Achilles tendon is called the myotendinous junction (MTJ). The gastrocnemius inserts at a higher point on the tendon than the soleus, creating a spiral structure in the tendon itself â€” the soleus fibers rotate medially as they descend. This spiral contributes to the tendon's elastic energy storage capacity.

Tendon Structure: How Collagen Works

Understanding tendon structure explains why it takes 12â€“16 weeks to build meaningful strength â€” and why sudden overload causes rupture.

  • Collagen fibers are the primary structural unit. Type I collagen forms parallel, crimped bundles that can store and release elastic energy like a spring.
  • Fascicles are bundles of collagen fibers surrounded by endotenon sheaths.
  • The paratenon is the outer sheath surrounding the entire tendon, providing vascularity and nutrition.
  • Tenocytes (tendon cells) synthesize new collagen in response to mechanical loading â€” but only if the load is within a productive range.

When load exceeds what the collagen matrix can handle, micro-tears occur. If recovery is inadequate, degenerative changes accumulate â€” resulting in tendinopathy (disorganized collagen, neovascularization, weakened structure). A degenerated tendon can rupture under loads that a healthy tendon would easily handle.

Tendons adapt to load 5â€“10Ã— more slowly than muscles. When you start a new training program, your muscles strengthen in 4â€“6 weeks. Your tendons take 12â€“16 weeks minimum to show meaningful structural adaptation. This gap creates a high-risk window where your muscles are strong but your tendons haven't caught up.

Blood Supply & the Watershed Zone

The Achilles tendon's vulnerability to rupture is directly linked to its blood supply â€” or lack thereof in one critical area.

Tendon vascularity comes from three sources:

  1. The myotendinous junction â€” rich vascular supply from the calf muscles
  2. The paratenon â€” blood vessels in the outer sheath along the tendon body
  3. The osseotendinous junction â€” vascular supply at the heel bone insertion

The problem is that 2â€“6 cm above the heel bone, there is a zone of relative avascularity where blood supply from all three sources is minimal. This area â€” the watershed zone â€” is exactly where 70â€“80% of Achilles ruptures occur.

Poor blood supply means:

  • Slower collagen turnover and repair after micro-damage
  • Slower recovery from loading stress
  • Accumulation of degenerative changes over time
  • Higher susceptibility to complete failure under load

Biomechanical Function

The Achilles tendon serves two primary biomechanical functions in sport:

Force Transmission

Every push-off movement â€” walking, running, jumping, sprinting â€” requires the calf muscles to transmit force through the Achilles to the heel bone. The tendon acts as a rigid cable, transmitting pull efficiently.

Elastic Energy Storage and Return

This is the Achilles' superpower. During running, the tendon stretches under load (storing elastic energy), then recoils to release that energy during push-off. This spring mechanism returns 35â€“50% of the energy stored, dramatically reducing the metabolic cost of locomotion. Without it, walking and running would require vastly more muscular effort.

Tendon stiffness â€” built through loading â€” increases the efficiency of this elastic mechanism. A stiffer, stronger tendon stores and releases more energy per loading cycle.

The Achilles experiences extraordinary forces in sport â€” context that explains why sudden load spikes are so dangerous:

  • Sprinting: ~8â€“12Ã— bodyweight â€” the highest Achilles loading in sport

The tendon can handle these forces when it's healthy. The danger isn't the force itself â€” it's the combination of a degenerated or undertrained tendon encountering unexpected force: the return from off-season sprint, the competitive game after vacation, the maximal effort on cold legs. Structural preparedness is everything.

Why the Achilles Tears: The Mechanism

Ruptures typically follow this pattern:

  1. Chronic degenerative process: Accumulation of micro-damage in the watershed zone over months or years. Often asymptomatic. Detected retrospectively after rupture â€” most ruptured tendons show pre-existing degeneration on histology.
  2. Acute overload event: A sudden, high-force movement (sprint takeoff, jumping, pivoting) applies force beyond the compromised tendon's capacity.
  3. Failure propagation: The weakened area can't arrest crack propagation. Full rupture occurs in milliseconds.

This is why most tears happen with a single "normal" movement â€” the tendon was already compromised. The event was just the tipping point.

Implications for Prevention & Training

Understanding Achilles anatomy leads directly to better training decisions:

  • Train the soleus specifically. Seated calf raises with knee bent at 90Â°. It's the neglected half of the Achilles complex and critical at running speeds.
  • Respect tendon adaptation timescales. When starting a new program or increasing training load, give the tendon 12â€“16 weeks to catch up with muscle strength gains.
  • Protect the watershed zone. Load progression should always be gradual â€” no sudden spikes. The watershed zone has the least capacity to self-repair quickly.
  • Build stiffness, not just strength. Isometric holds build tendon stiffness â€” enhancing the elastic energy return mechanism and reducing injury risk.
  • Train through full range of motion. Eccentric heel drops through the full range address both mid-substance and (with modification) insertional portions of the tendon.
  • Never train on a cold Achilles without warm-up. Cold tendons are stiffer, have less elastic capacity, and are less resilient to rapid loading. Warm-up is anatomically critical.
