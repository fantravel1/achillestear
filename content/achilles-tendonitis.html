The key insight: Achilles tendinopathy is NOT a simple inflammation problem. It's a structural change in the tendon that requires progressive loading to reverse â€” not rest, not stretching, not anti-inflammatories alone. This changes everything about how it's treated.

Tendonitis vs. Tendinopathy: What's the Difference?

You've probably heard both terms. Here's why the distinction matters for your treatment:

Tendonitis (Old Term)

  • Implies active inflammation
  • Treatment: rest, ice, NSAIDs
  • Expected to resolve in weeks
  • Accurate for acute injuries

Tendinopathy (Modern Term)

  • Degenerative change in tendon fibers
  • Treatment: progressive loading
  • Resolves over months with correct treatment
  • What most chronic Achilles pain actually is

Biopsies of chronically painful Achilles tendons consistently show absent or minimal inflammation â€” instead showing disorganized collagen, increased vascularity (neovascularization), and degenerative changes. This is why prolonged rest and anti-inflammatories fail to fix chronic tendon pain. The tendon needs load to remodel.

Two Types: Insertional vs. Mid-Substance

Location determines treatment approach. These are two different conditions:

Mid-Substance Tendinopathy

  • Pain 2â€“6 cm above heel bone
  • Most common (55â€“65% of cases)
  • Royal London Hospital Test positive
  • Responds well to eccentric loading
  • Often seen in runners & ball sport athletes

Insertional Tendinopathy

  • Pain at the heel bone attachment
  • 35â€“45% of cases
  • Compressive component involved
  • Eccentric loading in painful range avoided
  • Common in older athletes, heel-bone-spur patients

Note for insertional tendinopathy: Standard eccentric heel drops (going below neutral) can worsen insertional tendinopathy by increasing compression at the heel bone attachment. Work with a physiotherapist for appropriate modifications.

Causes & Risk Factors

Tendinopathy develops when the load on the tendon repeatedly exceeds its capacity to adapt. Risk factors include:

  • Training load spikes â€” increasing mileage, intensity, or frequency too fast (the most common cause)
  • Inadequate tendon strength â€” the calf/Achilles complex was never systematically loaded
  • Age â€” tendon collagen quality declines from age 30+
  • Metabolic factors â€” obesity, diabetes, and high cholesterol are strongly associated
  • Reduced ankle dorsiflexion â€” limited range increases tendon stress during activity
  • Footwear changes â€” switching to minimalist shoes or zero-drop without gradual adaptation
  • Fluoroquinolone antibiotics â€” can directly damage tendon tissue
  • Surface changes â€” switching from soft to hard surfaces increases cumulative load

Symptoms & Self-Assessment

Classic Symptom Pattern

  • Morning stiffness at the back of the ankle that eases with the first few steps
  • Pain at the start of activity that "warms up" and improves
  • Pain that returns after activity or the next morning
  • Tenderness when you pinch the tendon 2â€“6 cm above the heel
  • Possible thickening or palpable nodule in the tendon
  • Reduced capacity â€” can't sprint, jump, or push off as hard

The Royal London Hospital Test

Pinch the Achilles tendon on its sides (not front/back), about 3â€“5 cm above the heel. If this is painful, the test is positive â€” a strong indicator of mid-substance tendinopathy. If the pain changes or disappears when you dorsiflex the ankle (pull foot up), this is a highly specific finding.

The biggest mistake athletes make with tendinopathy is binary thinking: either training through all pain or stopping completely. Neither is optimal. Use this evidence-based model:

Treatment: What Works & What Doesn't

What Works

  • Progressive tendon loading â€” the core treatment. Structured eccentric, isometric, and heavy slow resistance exercises. See below.
  • Load modification â€” not elimination. Reduce provocative activities by 20â€“30% while maintaining structured loading.
  • Heel lifts (temporary) â€” 1â€“2 cm heel raise in shoes reduces Achilles load. Useful short-term while loading progresses.
  • NSAIDs (short-term) â€” can help in the acute phase (<6 weeks) but do not address the underlying issue long-term.
  • Physiotherapy â€” an experienced clinician can guide loading, identify contributing factors, and manage the condition effectively.

What Doesn't Work (or Has Limited Evidence)

  • Complete rest â€” tendons deteriorate without load. Pain returns when activity resumes.
  • Passive stretching alone â€” increases compressive load on the tendon insertion. Contraindicated for insertional tendinopathy.
  • Corticosteroid injections â€” provide short-term pain relief but are associated with tendon degeneration and higher rupture risk with repeated use.
  • Ultrasound therapy â€” limited evidence for tendinopathy.
  • PRP injections â€” emerging evidence but no consistent superiority over structured loading programs.

Return to Sport

There's no fixed calendar for return to sport. Use these objective criteria:

  • Single-leg calf raise endurance >90% of unaffected side (e.g., 25+ reps)
  • Single-leg hop test >90% of unaffected side
  • Morning VAS pain <2/10 on 3 consecutive days
  • No pain during or after a progressive running program over 4 weeks

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does Achilles tendinopathy take to heal?

With consistent, structured loading, most cases see significant improvement in 8â€“12 weeks. Complete resolution may take 3â€“6 months. The key variable is consistency â€” athletes who do their loading program daily progress much faster than those who do it sporadically.
