What Is an Achilles Tendon Tear?

The Achilles tendon is the thickest and strongest tendon in the human body â€” yet it's also the most commonly ruptured. It connects your calf muscles (gastrocnemius and soleus) to the heel bone (calcaneus) and is responsible for every push-off movement: walking, running, jumping, and sprinting.

An Achilles tendon tear (also called an Achilles rupture) occurs when the tendon fibers partially or completely separate, usually at the "watershed zone" â€” a point approximately 2â€“6 cm above the heel bone where blood supply is naturally reduced. This area is structurally vulnerable and is where the vast majority of tears occur.

Key insight: Most Achilles tears don't happen because of one traumatic event. They occur because of accumulated tendon stress over time â€” often with a pre-existing degenerative process â€” and then a single explosive movement becomes the "final straw."

Complete vs. Partial Rupture

Not all Achilles tears are the same. The severity determines treatment options and recovery timeline.

Complete Rupture

  • All tendon fibers severed
  • Immediate inability to push off
  • Positive Thompson test
  • Gap visible/palpable
  • Surgery often discussed
  • 6â€“12 month recovery

Partial Tear

  • Some fibers intact
  • Painful but some function preserved
  • Negative or equivocal Thompson
  • No clear gap
  • Usually managed non-surgically
  • 3â€“6 month recovery

Insertional vs. Mid-Substance Tears

Tears can also be classified by location:

  • Mid-substance tears (most common, ~80%) occur in the watershed zone 2â€“6 cm above the heel.
  • Insertional tears occur at the point where the tendon attaches to the heel bone. More common in older athletes and those with heel spurs.

Causes & Risk Factors

Achilles tendon tears don't happen randomly. They follow predictable patterns driven by specific risk factors.

Immediate Causes

The typical mechanism is a sudden explosive force applied to the tendon â€” usually:

  • Explosive push-off (sprinting, jumping, lunging)
  • Sudden dorsiflexion (tripping on a step, landing awkwardly)
  • Direct trauma (tackle, collision)

Underlying Risk Factors

  • Age (30â€“50): Tendon collagen degenerates with age. The 35â€“45 range has the highest rupture incidence â€” particularly in "weekend warriors."
  • Male sex: Men rupture the Achilles 5â€“8x more often than women in recreational sport.
  • Previous tendinopathy: A degenerative tendon is 4â€“10x more likely to rupture.
  • Sudden load spikes: Starting a new sport, returning from off-season, or doubling training volume.
  • Fluoroquinolone antibiotics: (Ciprofloxacin, Levofloxacin) increase rupture risk up to 3x. Always ask your doctor about alternatives.
  • Corticosteroid injections: Into or near the tendon significantly increase rupture risk.
  • Poor footwear: Flat shoes after years of heeled shoes cause sudden Achilles lengthening stress.
  • Inadequate warm-up: Cold tendons are stiffer and less resilient to rapid loading.

Symptoms: How to Recognize a Tear

At the Moment of Injury

A complete Achilles rupture is usually unmistakable:

  • Sudden, severe pain at the back of the ankle or calf
  • An audible "pop" or "snap" â€” often loud enough for bystanders to hear
  • Feeling like being "shot" or "kicked" from behind â€” even with no contact
  • Immediate inability to push off or bear weight normally
  • Rapid swelling and bruising

The Thompson Test (Self-Check)

Lie face down with your foot hanging off a table or bed. Have someone squeeze your calf muscle. In a healthy Achilles, the foot will plantarflex (point down). If the foot doesn't move, the tendon is likely ruptured. This is called a positive Thompson test.

Important: Do not attempt to diagnose yourself or "walk it off." If you suspect an Achilles rupture, seek emergency care immediately. Imaging (ultrasound or MRI) is needed for accurate diagnosis and treatment planning.

Symptoms of Partial Tears / Tendinopathy

Partial tears and degenerative tendinopathy often present more gradually:

  • Morning stiffness in the Achilles that "warms up" during activity
  • Tenderness to touch at the tendon (2â€“6 cm above heel)
  • Pain at the start of activity that improves then worsens
  • Thickening or nodule in the tendon
  • Reduced push-off power

Diagnosis

Achilles tears are diagnosed through clinical examination and imaging:

  • Thompson test: High sensitivity (96%) for complete ruptures.
  • Ultrasound: First-line imaging â€” fast, inexpensive, dynamic. Can identify complete vs. partial tears and gap size.
  • MRI: Gold standard for detailed assessment, particularly for partial tears, insertional pathology, or pre-surgical planning.
  • X-ray: Rules out associated heel fractures but does not image the tendon itself.

Treatment: Surgery vs. Non-Surgical

This is the most debated question in Achilles care. Modern research suggests that for many patients, outcomes are equivalent.

Surgical Treatment

  • Tendon ends stitched together
  • Lower re-rupture rate (~3â€“5%)
  • Faster early strength return
  • Risks: infection, nerve damage, scar
  • Preferred for younger athletes, large gap, high-level sport

Non-Surgical (Functional Rehab)

  • Boot immobilization + early mobilization
  • Re-rupture rate ~8â€“12%
  • No surgical risks
  • Similar 1-year outcomes to surgery
  • Preferred for older patients, lower activity, medical comorbidities

The 2020 UKSTAR trial and multiple meta-analyses confirm that with a structured early functional rehabilitation protocol, non-surgical treatment achieves equivalent outcomes to surgery for most patients at 1 year. Discuss with your orthopedic surgeon which is appropriate for your specific situation.

Preventing Re-Rupture & Future Tears

After an Achilles tear, the risk of re-rupture and contralateral rupture is significantly elevated. Prevention is non-negotiable.

  • Eccentric heel drops â€” daily, forever. The single most evidence-backed exercise for Achilles tendon health.
  • Progressive loading â€” never spike training volume by more than 10% per week.
  • Structured warm-up â€” including calf pumps, ankle mobilization, and pogos before every session.
  • Strength program â€” 12-week tendon strength protocol to rebuild capacity beyond pre-injury levels.
  • Load monitoring â€” track weekly training load and use deload weeks every 4â€“6 weeks.
  • Watch for warning signs â€” morning stiffness, activity-related pain, or tendon thickening should trigger immediate load reduction and assessment.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does an Achilles tendon tear feel like?

A complete rupture feels like a sudden sharp pop or snap at the back of the ankle â€” often described as being "kicked" or "shot" from behind, even with no contact. It is immediately followed by severe pain, inability to push off or stand on tiptoe, and visible swelling. Many people hear an audible snap.

Can you walk with a torn Achilles?

With a complete rupture, normal walking is severely compromised. You cannot rise on your toes and push-off is nearly impossible. However, some people can still walk with a limp using a flat-footed gait. Partial tears allow more function but still cause significant pain and weakness. Don't use this ability to walk as a gauge of severity.

Surgery or no surgery for Achilles tear?

Research shows similar long-term outcomes for surgical vs. non-surgical treatment of Achilles tendon ruptures. Surgery may offer a slightly lower re-rupture rate (3â€“5% vs 8â€“12%) but carries surgical risks including infection and nerve damage. The decision depends on age, activity level, rupture gap size, and patient preference. Discuss with your orthopedic surgeon.

How long is Achilles recovery really?

Full return to sport typically takes 6â€“12 months. Return to walking without a boot happens by 8â€“12 weeks. Many athletes underestimate recovery time â€” the tendon feels better before it's structurally ready for explosive loading. Do not rush this.

Can an Achilles tear heal without surgery?

Yes. For many patients, particularly those over 40 or with lower activity demands, non-surgical treatment with a functional rehabilitation protocol produces excellent outcomes comparable to surgery at 1 year. The key is strict adherence to a structured boot protocol and progressive rehabilitation.
