The Science of Eccentric Loading:
Why Slow Reps Rebuild Tendons

February 12, 2026 ¬∑ 7 min read ¬∑ AchillesTear.com

In the late 1990s, a Swedish orthopaedic surgeon named Hakan Alfredson found himself at an impasse. Suffering from chronic Achilles tendinopathy, he wanted the surgical treatment his own hospital offered ‚Äî but his chief would only approve the operation if conservative care had first been exhausted. Alfredson's solution was characteristically audacious: he designed his own eccentric loading protocol, performed it on himself until the pain became intolerable, and then ‚Äî when his tendon stubbornly refused to rupture ‚Äî discovered it had actually healed instead. The 1998 paper that followed,1 showing an 80% success rate in chronic Achilles tendinopathy with a structured 12-week eccentric program, rewrote the rehabilitation playbook for an entire generation of clinicians and athletes.

But why does it work? Why does the lowering phase of a heel drop ‚Äî not the rising ‚Äî appear to drive structural repair in a tissue that has resisted months of rest, anti-inflammatories, and gentle stretching? The answer sits at the intersection of biomechanics, cell biology, and the peculiar anatomy of the Achilles tendon itself. Understanding that answer will change the way you approach every phase of your own recovery.

Eccentric vs. Concentric: More Than a Direction

Every muscle contraction falls into one of three categories. A concentric contraction shortens the muscle as it produces force ‚Äî the upward phase of a calf raise, for instance, where the gastrocnemius and soleus shorten to plantarflex the foot. An isometric contraction holds length constant while generating tension, like pressing a foot against a wall. An eccentric contraction is different and, for tendons, therapeutically distinct: the muscle lengthens under load, producing braking force against a resistance greater than the muscle itself can overcome concentrically.

In a standard eccentric heel drop on a step, you use both legs to rise to the balls of your feet, then shift all your weight to the affected side and lower yourself over three to four seconds ‚Äî resisting gravity all the way down until the heel sinks below step level. The calf complex is under near-maximal tension while the tendon elongates. That combination of high load and tissue deformation is the key signal.

Key Takeaway

Eccentric loading generates substantially greater mechanical tension in the tendon than concentric loading at the same speed. This tension ‚Äî not the movement direction per se ‚Äî is the primary stimulus for tenoblast activation, collagen synthesis, and structural remodeling. The slow tempo amplifies time-under-tension and ensures the load is controlled, not ballistic.

The Alfredson Protocol: What the Original Study Actually Showed

Alfredson et al. (1998)1 enrolled 15 recreational athletes with chronic Achilles tendinopathy who had failed at least three months of conventional treatment. The protocol was simple and brutal: three sets of 15 repetitions on a stair edge, performed with a straight knee (loading the gastrocnemius) and then with a bent knee (isolating the deeper soleus), twice daily, seven days a week, for 12 weeks. When exercises became pain-free, participants added load using a backpack until they reached bodyweight plus 10‚Äì30 kg.

All 15 patients returned to their pre-injury running levels. A matched control group who declined the program all went on to surgery. Subsequent trials replicated the roughly 80% clinical success rate across different patient populations, cementing the eccentric heel drop as the single most evidence-supported conservative intervention in Achilles tendinopathy management.

It is worth being precise about what "success" means here. The Alfredson paper measured return to sport, not tendon appearance on imaging. This distinction matters: tendinopathic tendons often remain structurally abnormal on ultrasound even after full symptomatic resolution. The goal of eccentric loading is not to restore the tendon to its original architecture ‚Äî it is to shift the tissue's mechanical properties enough that it can tolerate athletic load without producing pain and further disrepair.

Mechanical Tension and the Biology of Repair

Tendons are composed primarily of Type I collagen ‚Äî long, hierarchically arranged fibrils that resist tensile force with extraordinary efficiency. In a healthy tendon, these fibrils are densely packed and aligned along the tendon's long axis. In a tendinopathic tendon, that architecture is disrupted: collagen fibers become disorganized, neo-vascularization appears, and accompanying nerve fibers explain much of the pain signal.

The cells responsible for synthesizing and remodeling collagen are called tenocytes (mature) and tenoblasts (progenitor cells). These cells are mechanosensitive ‚Äî they respond to mechanical strain by upregulating collagen synthesis pathways. The signal travels through integrin receptors on the cell surface, which detect extracellular matrix deformation and transduce it into intracellular signaling cascades involving focal adhesion kinase (FAK), Rho GTPases, and ultimately transcription factors that drive Type I collagen gene expression.

Eccentric loading appears to produce the magnitude and rate of tendon strain that optimally stimulates this pathway. Concentric loading at equivalent speeds generates lower peak tendon stress ‚Äî partly because the Achilles is pre-loaded during the stance phase before the concentric impulse begins, and partly because the braking demand of eccentric contraction requires greater total muscle force output. Several in vivo studies using ultrasound tissue tracking have confirmed that Achilles tendon strain is measurably higher during the eccentric phase of a controlled heel drop than during the concentric rise.

The Watershed Zone: Why Tendons Heal Slowly

Even with optimal loading, Achilles tendons take a frustratingly long time to remodel. The reason lies in a region approximately 2‚Äì6 cm above the calcaneal insertion known as the watershed zone ‚Äî the area where the tendon's blood supply is most sparse. Unlike muscle, which is richly vascularized, mature tendon derives most of its nutrition from diffusion through synovial fluid and from the peritendinous tissues. The central core of the Achilles at the watershed zone receives very little direct arterial supply, which is why tendinopathy and rupture cluster in this precise anatomical location.

Poor perfusion means that collagen-synthesizing cells in this region have limited access to the amino acid substrates, growth factors, and oxygen they need to produce new matrix efficiently. Tenoblasts require a minimum metabolic supply to function, and when that supply is insufficient ‚Äî as it is in the watershed zone at rest ‚Äî collagen synthesis slows dramatically. This is one reason why passive rest alone rarely resolves midportion tendinopathy: the tissue lacks the metabolic and mechanical stimulus to drive meaningful remodeling.

Once collagen synthesis is triggered by mechanical loading, the newly synthesized collagen requires approximately 100 days to mature through a process of cross-linking and fibril organization. This timeline explains why protocols shorter than 12 weeks tend to produce less durable outcomes, and why athletes who feel better at six weeks and return to full training often relapse ‚Äî the new collagen is biochemically immature and mechanically weaker than the surrounding tissue.

Isometric, Eccentric, or Heavy Slow Resistance: When to Use Each

A significant refinement of tendinopathy management came with Cook and Purdam's tendon continuum model (2009).2 Rather than treating tendinopathy as a single, uniform condition, Cook and Purdam proposed a spectrum from reactive tendinopathy (acutely irritable, often swollen) through tendon disrepair (early structural change) to degenerative tendinopathy (established matrix disruption). Critically, each stage responds differently to loading, and choosing the wrong type at the wrong time can drive the tendon further along the continuum.

Isometric loading ‚Äî sustained holds against an immovable resistance ‚Äî is the appropriate starting point for reactive or highly irritable tendons. A typical isometric protocol involves pushing the ball of the foot against a wall or floor, holding 45‚Äì60 seconds at 70‚Äì80% of maximum voluntary contraction, repeated four to five times. Isometric contractions produce tendon stress without the repetitive strain cycling that provokes reactive flares, and there is growing evidence that they also produce centrally mediated analgesia ‚Äî temporary pain reduction that allows subsequent therapeutic exercise to proceed more comfortably.

Eccentric loading (the Alfredson approach) becomes appropriate once the acute irritability has settled ‚Äî typically when resting pain has resolved and the tendon is no longer reactive to gentle palpation. This is where the majority of clinical benefit accrues for midportion tendinopathy, and where the collagen synthesis stimulus is most reliably produced.

Heavy slow resistance (HSR) training ‚Äî concentric and eccentric loading combined, performed very slowly (typically 3 seconds up, 3 seconds down), with high loads and low repetitions ‚Äî has emerged as an alternative to pure eccentric protocols, particularly for athletes who find the daily Alfredson program impractical to sustain. A landmark 2015 randomized controlled trial by Beyer et al.3 found HSR to be equally effective as the Alfredson eccentric protocol at 12 weeks, with superior patient satisfaction. HSR has the additional advantage of building concentric calf strength, which the pure eccentric protocol does not meaningfully address ‚Äî an important consideration for athletes who need to produce force, not just absorb it.

The "No Pain, No Gain" Nuance

One of the most clinically important ‚Äî and most misunderstood ‚Äî aspects of eccentric loading protocols is the deliberate acceptance of some pain during exercise. The original Alfredson protocol explicitly instructed participants to continue through discomfort, with the caveat that sharp, escalating pain warranted stopping. Modern guidelines have refined this into a practical monitoring tool: a pain level of 3‚Äì4 out of 10 on a numeric rating scale during eccentric exercise is acceptable, provided that pain returns to baseline or better within 24 hours of the session.

This is a departure from the "if it hurts, stop" approach that governs most injury rehabilitation contexts. The rationale is that tendons, unlike muscles and joints, do not produce inflammatory pain signals that reliably indicate tissue damage. A chronically irritable Achilles tendon may produce a 4/10 pain response to any loaded activity ‚Äî and if that threshold were observed as a stop signal, no loading would ever occur and the tendon would never be exposed to the mechanical stimulus it needs. The 24-hour rule provides a safer monitoring framework: if pain is still elevated the following morning, the previous session was too aggressive and load should be reduced.

Pain Monitoring Rule

During eccentric loading for tendinopathy, use this simple traffic-light system during and after each session:

  • 0‚Äì3/10 pain during exercise, resolved next morning: Load is appropriate. Maintain or gradually progress.
  • 4/10 pain during exercise, resolved next morning: Acceptable. Do not increase load yet.
  • Above 5/10 during exercise, or lingering next morning: Too much. Reduce sets, reps, or resistance for the next session.
  • Sharp, sudden, or worsening pain: Stop immediately. Rule out partial tear before continuing.

A few critical execution points that are frequently botched in practice:

Use a step, not the floor. The heel must drop below the level of the forefoot to achieve full range of motion and maximize tendon strain in the position where loading is most therapeutically relevant. Performing heel drops on flat ground eliminates the eccentric range entirely.

Both the gastrocnemius and soleus must be trained separately. The Achilles tendon receives input from both muscles. Straight-knee drops load the gastrocnemius predominantly; bent-knee drops shift emphasis to the soleus. Omitting the bent-knee variation is one of the most common errors in self-directed programs and leaves a significant portion of the tendon under-stimulated.

Progress load, not speed. When 3 √ó 15 becomes pain-free, add external load rather than speeding up the tempo. Increasing tempo reduces time-under-tension and the mechanical stimulus. Keep the 3‚Äì4 second lowering tempo throughout the loading phase.

Bilateral loading on the way up is not cheating ‚Äî it is essential. The eccentric protocol isolates the mechanical stress to the lowering phase on purpose. Using both legs to rise protects the tendon from ballistic concentric loading that could provoke a reactive flare in the early phases, and ensures the energy for the eccentric is not being wasted on a concentric effort the injured limb cannot safely perform.

Beyond the Alfredson Protocol: What the Evidence Has Evolved to Show

The 25 years of research since Alfredson's original paper have refined several assumptions embedded in the original protocol. Twice-daily loading was empirically derived ‚Äî Alfredson chose it because that was what he did to himself, and it worked. More recent evidence suggests that once-daily eccentric loading produces comparable outcomes with better adherence, particularly in the later phases when volume is accumulating alongside sport-specific training.

The role of tendon blood flow has also been re-examined. Earlier models assumed that neo-vascularization in tendinopathic tendons was pathological and that reducing it (via sclerosing injections, for instance) would resolve pain. The Cook and Purdam continuum model2 reframed this: neo-vascularization may be an adaptive response to the tendon's poor intrinsic blood supply, and eliminating it without addressing the underlying mechanical deficiency tends not to produce durable results. The more productive target is improving the mechanical quality of the matrix, which eccentric and HSR loading accomplishes by upregulating collagen synthesis while also mechanically aligning newly synthesized fibrils along the line of force.

There is also growing interest in the role of tendon compliance ‚Äî its elastic stiffness ‚Äî as a biomarker of rehabilitation progress. Shear wave elastography studies suggest that effective eccentric loading progressively increases tendon stiffness toward the values seen in healthy contralateral tendons. This is mechanically beneficial: a stiffer tendon transfers force from muscle to bone more efficiently and stores elastic energy more effectively during the stretch-shortening cycle of running and jumping. Athletes returning to sport with persistently compliant (soft) tendons on the affected side are at higher re-injury risk, which underscores why the later HSR and plyometric phases of the protocol are not optional even when pain has resolved.

Clinical Bottom Line

Eccentric loading works because it delivers mechanical tension to mechanosensitive tenocytes, driving Type I collagen synthesis in a tissue that has a poor intrinsic blood supply and cannot self-repair through rest alone. The slow tempo is not incidental ‚Äî it is the mechanism. Collagen maturation takes approximately 100 days, which is why 12-week protocols are a minimum, not a target. Pain of 3‚Äì4/10 during loading is acceptable and does not indicate harm, provided it resolves within 24 hours.

If you are managing a diagnosed tendinopathy, work with a physiotherapist to confirm staging, rule out insertional pathology (which responds differently to eccentric loading), and establish an appropriate starting load before beginning any self-directed protocol.
