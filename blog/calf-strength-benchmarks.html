Calf Strength Benchmarks: How Do You Stack Up?

February 4, 2026 6 min read

When researchers and clinicians look for the single most modifiable risk factor for Achilles tendon injury, the conversation does not start with flexibility, footwear, or running surface. It starts with calf strength. Specifically, the capacity of the gastrocnemius and soleus complex to repeatedly absorb and generate force during the propulsive phase of gait and during the rapid load-unload cycles of jumping and direction change.

Ankle flexibility is frequently blamed for Achilles problems, and while restricted dorsiflexion does contribute to altered mechanics, it is rarely the root cause of injury on its own. The more consistent finding across epidemiological studies is that athletes with inadequate plantarflexion strength — even those with no symptoms — carry a meaningfully elevated risk of tendinopathy and rupture. The challenge is that most people have no idea where they sit relative to a meaningful standard. This article fixes that.

Why Calf Strength Is the Number One Modifiable Risk Factor

The Achilles tendon transmits forces equivalent to 6 to 8 times bodyweight during running and up to 12 times bodyweight during explosive jumping tasks. The tendon itself is a passive structure — it stores and releases elastic energy, but it cannot generate force. All the protective loading must come from the calf musculature contracting eccentrically to control tendon elongation and concentrically to drive propulsion.

When calf strength is insufficient for the demands being placed on the tendon, two things happen. First, the load per unit of tendon tissue increases — the tendon is asked to do more with less muscular buffering. Second, compensatory movement patterns emerge: the hip extensors, knee flexors, and even lumbar extensors begin to pick up the slack, changing the direction and magnitude of force applied to the Achilles. Both mechanisms increase cumulative tendon stress.

The practical implication is straightforward: building calf strength to and beyond the sport-specific benchmarks below is the most direct, evidence-supported action available to any athlete who wants to reduce Achilles injury risk. The 3 Benchmark Tests

Test 1: Single-Leg Calf Raise Endurance (Silbernagel et al.)

This is the most widely validated and clinically used measure of functional plantarflexion capacity. The protocol, established by Silbernagel and colleagues, involves standing on one leg with the knee straight, rising fully onto the ball of the foot, and lowering under control. Repetitions continue until form breaks down — specifically, until the athlete can no longer achieve full range of motion or begins to compensate by bending the knee or leaning the trunk. Test 2: Seated Calf Raise Load Test

The seated calf raise targets the soleus in isolation — the deeper, predominantly slow-twitch muscle that is the primary load-bearer during walking and sustained running. Because the soleus crosses only the ankle (not the knee), seated testing with the knee bent at 90 degrees effectively bypasses the gastrocnemius and isolates the muscle that matters most for tendinopathy prevention in endurance athletes.

The baseline standard, drawn from rehabilitation medicine literature, is the ability to complete 3 sets of 15 repetitions with a load equal to 1 times bodyweight on a seated calf raise machine. An 80kg athlete should be able to complete 3x15 with 80kg of machine load. This sounds demanding because it is — the soleus is capable of generating very high forces, and most athletes are substantially undertrained in this range. Access our seated calf raise guide for setup instructions, particularly regarding foot position and range of motion standardization.

Test 3: Force Plate Peak Plantarflexion Force

For athletes with access to a force plate or sports performance laboratory, peak plantarflexion force provides the most objective single-number snapshot of the calf's capacity to generate propulsive force. The test is typically administered as a maximal isometric or explosive heel raise from a standardized position, with force normalized to bodyweight.

The benchmark for recreational to club-level athletes is a peak plantarflexion force of 2.5 to 3 times bodyweight. Elite sprint and jump athletes routinely exceed 4 times bodyweight. Values below 2.5x bodyweight indicate that the calf musculature is not prepared for the loading demands of most competitive sports and that the Achilles is being asked to manage force it is not adequately buffered against.

Sport-Specific Strength Standards

The 25-rep threshold is a minimum baseline, not a performance target. The demands on the Achilles vary substantially by sport, and training to the minimum leaves athletes with little reserve capacity when training loads spike — during pre-season intensification, tournament schedules, or rapid return from a break. The table below gives sport-specific targets.

The Calf Strength Gap: Why You Probably Have a Deficit You Don't Know About

One of the most consistent findings in sports medicine is that a significant bilateral calf strength asymmetry exists in a large proportion of active people — with a deficit of 20 to 35% in the weaker leg being common. This gap is not just present in people who have had previous injury: it is found frequently in athletes with no injury history, often as a legacy of subtle movement asymmetries, previous ankle sprains, or simply the dominant-leg bias present in most sport skills.

The insidious nature of this deficit is that it rarely causes discomfort at low-to-moderate training intensities. The Achilles compensates silently, accumulating load until a threshold is crossed — typically during a spike in volume or intensity. By the time symptoms appear, the tendon is often already in a reactive tendinopathy state.

Testing both legs individually is therefore not optional. Always record the score for each leg separately. A score of 30 reps on your dominant leg and 19 on your non-dominant leg is not a passing grade — it is a 37% asymmetry that needs to be addressed systematically before it becomes a clinical problem.

Self-Assessment: The Home Version of the Benchmark

Single-Leg Calf Raise Test — At Home

  1. Setup: Stand barefoot on a flat, firm surface. Place one hand lightly on a wall or doorframe for balance only — do not use your arm to assist the movement. Lift the non-testing foot off the floor and hold it slightly behind the supporting leg.
  2. Movement: Rise fully onto the ball of the foot, achieving maximum heel elevation. Lower slowly and under control until the heel is just below floor level if using a step, or until the foot is flat if on the floor. Each repetition should take approximately 2 seconds up and 2 seconds down.
  3. Termination: Stop counting when you can no longer achieve full height on the rise, when you begin bending the knee more than slightly, or when you need to push with your hand for assistance.
  4. Record: Note the total reps for each leg. Test the uninjured or less symptomatic leg first.
  5. Calculate your deficit: (Weaker leg reps ÷ stronger leg reps) × 100 = Symmetry percentage. Below 90% indicates a meaningful asymmetry requiring targeted correction.

Testing tip: For the most accurate and comparable results, perform this test at the same time of day on both occasions, after a 5-minute warm-up walk but before any fatiguing exercise. Do not test after a heavy leg training session. Re-test every 4 weeks to track progress.

4-Week Rapid Strength Protocol (Below-Benchmark Score)

If you score below 15 reps (high risk) or 15–24 reps (moderate risk), the protocol below is structured to produce measurable improvement within 4 weeks. It combines the eccentric-concentric method (which provides the strongest stimulus for both muscle hypertrophy and tendon collagen synthesis) with progressive volume increases. Stop if pain exceeds 3/10 on the VAS during or after any session.

Week 1 — Foundation (Flat Surface)

3 sets of 10 single-leg heel raises on flat ground, both legs, twice daily. Slow and controlled: 2 seconds up, 2 second hold at top, 3 seconds down. Rest 90 seconds between sets. Focus on achieving maximum height on every repetition.

Week 2 — Range Increase (Step Edge)

Transition to the edge of a step, allowing the heel to drop below the level of the ball of the foot at the bottom. 3 sets of 12 reps each leg, twice daily. The increased range of motion at the bottom substantially increases the eccentric stimulus and is the primary driver of tendon adaptation. Use a secure step with a railing for balance.

Week 3 — Volume and Tempo Increase

4 sets of 15 reps from the step, once daily. Increase to a 4-second eccentric (lowering) phase. Introduce a small amount of external load if 4x15 feels manageable: hold a light dumbbell (5–10kg) or wear a loaded backpack on the weaker side only to address the asymmetry.

Week 4 — Consolidation and Retest

4 sets of 20 reps from the step with the same tempo as Week 3. Add external load to the stronger leg to begin equalizing the training stimulus. At the end of Week 4, retest both legs using the protocol above and compare to your Week 0 score. Most athletes improve by 6–10 reps within a 4-week structured protocol.

After completing this protocol, progress to the sport-specific standards outlined in the table above. For ongoing structured programming, visit the exercise library for complete tendon loading sequences, and refer to our return-to-sport criteria guide if you are working back from a prior injury.

The Bottom Line

Calf strength is not a performance metric that sits at the margins of injury prevention — it is central to it. The standards described here are not arbitrary: they reflect the force demands of sport and the minimum muscular capacity required to buffer the Achilles tendon against those demands. Test both legs, record the numbers honestly, and use the deficit as a training target rather than a source of concern. A weak calf is a fixable problem. An Achilles rupture is not.
