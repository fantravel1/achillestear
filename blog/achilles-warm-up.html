The 8-Minute Achilles Warm-Up Every Athlete Needs

February 10, 2026 • 5 min read • Category: Prevention

Most athletes warm up their muscles. Almost none warm up their tendons ‚Äî and that distinction is exactly why Achilles injuries happen in the first five minutes of training, not the forty-fifth. If you have ever felt a sudden sharp pull during an early sprint or a first explosive cut, you have experienced this gap first-hand.

This guide gives you an evidence-based, eight-minute protocol that progresses through three distinct phases: mobilize, load, and react. Every exercise has a physiological reason for being in the sequence, and every transition matters. Run through this before every session, regardless of sport, and you will have done more for your Achilles longevity than most athletes manage in years of training.

Why Cold Tendons Tear

Tendons are viscoelastic structures, meaning their mechanical behavior changes with temperature. At rest and at lower tissue temperatures, collagen fibrils within the tendon are more tightly cross-linked and the surrounding proteoglycan matrix is more viscous. Research consistently shows that a resting tendon can be up to ten times stiffer than a fully warmed tendon at equivalent loads.

Viscosity is the critical variable here. A high-viscosity tendon cannot absorb and redistribute stress efficiently across its cross-section. Instead, load concentrates at localized points ‚Äî particularly at the mid-substance and at the tendon's insertion on the calcaneus ‚Äî creating peak stress far above what the structure can safely tolerate. When a cold athlete sprints hard off the line, that concentrated stress spike can exceed the tendon's failure threshold before any protective muscle activation has a chance to kick in.

Warming the tissue does two things simultaneously: it lowers viscosity so the tendon behaves more like the elastic spring it is designed to be, and it increases the cross-sectional stiffness uniformly, which distributes load more evenly. The result is a tendon that can safely accept sudden, high-force demands. Eight minutes is enough to produce meaningful changes in tendon temperature and compliance if you use the right exercises in the right sequence.

Why Static Stretching Before Activity Is Wrong for the Achilles

⚠ Do Not Do This: Static Calf Stretch Before Training

Standing against a wall and holding a calf stretch for 30-60 seconds before a training session feels responsible. It is not. For the Achilles specifically, static end-range dorsiflexion stretching on a cold tendon applies compressive load at the calcaneal insertion ‚Äî exactly where insertional Achilles tendinopathy develops. The tendon is not yet warm enough to distribute that compressive stress, and you are applying it before any adaptive blood flow or temperature increase has occurred.

Static stretching also temporarily reduces muscle-tendon unit stiffness and peak force production, which means you walk onto the court or track with less reactive capacity than when you arrived. Save sustained stretching for your cool-down, when tissue temperature is elevated and you are not about to demand explosive output.

The research on pre-exercise static stretching has shifted substantially over the past decade. A 2024 meta-analysis of tendon loading studies confirmed that passive, sustained stretch on a cold tendon generates unfavorable compressive stress at the bone-tendon junction without providing the vascular or temperature benefits that actually prepare the tissue for load. Dynamic movement, low-level progressive loading, and plyometric preparation are the evidence-based alternative ‚Äî which is exactly what the three-phase protocol below delivers.

The 3-Phase Warm-Up Framework: Mobilize, Load, React

The sequence is non-negotiable. Each phase creates the preconditions for the next. You cannot skip straight to pogo hops on a cold, immobile ankle any more than you can safely sprint without first elevating your heart rate. The three phases are:

  • Phase 1 ‚Äî Mobilize (2 minutes): Restore full ankle range of motion, begin increasing tissue temperature through rhythmic movement, and activate synovial fluid production in the ankle joint.
  • Phase 2 ‚Äî Load (3 minutes): Introduce controlled tensile load to the tendon through progressive single-leg challenges and resisted dorsiflexion, raising tissue temperature and beginning neuromuscular activation of the calf-Achilles complex.
  • Phase 3 ‚Äî React (3 minutes): Prime the stretch-shortening cycle with low-amplitude plyometrics and direction-specific movement patterns that match the demands of your sport.

The Complete 8-Minute Protocol

Perform each exercise in order. Rest periods between exercises are built into the transition time ‚Äî move efficiently but do not rush. The entire sequence takes eight minutes when performed at a controlled pace.

Phase 1 in Detail: Mobilize (2 Minutes)

Phase 1

Mobilize ‚Äî Ankle Range of Motion and Fluid Movement

0:00 ‚Äì 2:00

Begin with ankle circles ‚Äî ten rotations in each direction on each foot. The goal is not to stretch anything; it is to begin circulating synovial fluid through the tibiotalar joint and to gently load the Achilles through a rhythmic range rather than a static hold. Follow immediately with the ankle alphabet, tracing every letter of the alphabet with your big toe. This exercise is underrated precisely because it is unglamorous: it challenges plantarflexion, dorsiflexion, inversion, and eversion in unpredictable sequences, waking up the proprioceptive pathways that protect the ankle during cutting movements. Finish with twenty slow calf pumps ‚Äî think of these as a hydraulic pump for the tendon, driving blood in and out of the relatively avascular mid-substance zone.

Phase 2 in Detail: Load (3 Minutes)

Phase 2

Load ‚Äî Single-Leg Stability and Resisted Dorsiflexion

2:00 ‚Äì 5:00

Thirty seconds of single-leg balance on each foot is the transition from joint mobility to load tolerance. The calf is working isometrically throughout, and the proprioceptive demand of balancing on one leg activates the entire calf-Achilles-plantar fascia chain in a controlled way. Add small quarter-squat pulses to increase load through the Achilles without approaching the compressive ranges of a full single-leg calf raise. The banded dorsiflexion exercise that follows is perhaps the most important single exercise in this sequence: by driving the knee forward against band resistance, you eccentrically load the calf while simultaneously training the ankle's range in a weight-bearing position ‚Äî the exact movement pattern most at-risk during early session sprinting. Twenty reps per ankle at a controlled two-second tempo is sufficient to produce a meaningful tendon temperature increase.

Phase 3 in Detail: React (3 Minutes)

Phase 3

React ‚Äî Plyometric Priming and Speed Progression

5:00 ‚Äì 8:00

Pogo hops are the safest entry point into plyometric loading because the amplitude is small and the body weight demand is modest relative to a full jumping exercise. Three sets of ten ‚Äî with genuinely stiff ankles and the shortest possible ground contact ‚Äî trains the stretch-shortening cycle at the tendon without the impact forces of a box jump or depth drop. This is enough to prime the elastic energy storage capacity of the Achilles for the session ahead. A-skips then introduce directional movement and a dorsiflexed foot-strike pattern that directly mimics running mechanics. The final progressive run-up ‚Äî starting at 50% effort, rising to 75% ‚Äî completes the preparation gradient. You arrive at your first full-speed effort having already loaded the tendon through a complete physiological ramp.

Adapting the Protocol for Your Sport

The eight-minute sequence is universal, but the emphasis within Phase 3 should reflect the mechanical demands of your specific sport. The mobilize and load phases remain identical across all sports ‚Äî the tendon biology does not care what you play.

Court Sports
  • Increase pogo hop volume to 4 sets
  • Add lateral pogo hops (side to side)
  • Replace run-ups with 3 shuttle-length change-of-direction runs at progressive speed
  • Emphasize quick ground contacts over distance
Distance Running
  • Extend progressive run-ups to 4 reps: 40%, 55%, 70%, 85%
  • Add 10 single-leg calf pumps per side between run-ups
  • Perform A-skips over 20m instead of 15m
  • Prioritise forward propulsion mechanics
Field Sports
  • Include 2 sets of lateral A-skips after standard A-skips
  • Add backward jogging over 10m to load the Achilles in a dorsiflexion-biased pattern
  • Progress run-ups to include one 75% curved-path sprint
  • Finish with 5 single-leg landings each side

For athletes returning from a period of reduced training ‚Äî whether due to illness, travel, or an extended rest ‚Äî reduce Phase 3 to one set of pogo hops and a single 50% run-up for the first two sessions back. The tendon deconditions faster than the muscle and needs a conservative ramp back to full preparation intensity.

Warning Signs to Watch for During the Warm-Up

The warm-up itself is a diagnostic tool. Pay attention to how the tendon responds during each phase ‚Äî your body is giving you information that your training history alone cannot provide.

⚠ Warning Signs During Warm-Up
  • Morning stiffness that does not resolve by Phase 2: If your ankle feels noticeably stiffer than usual after completing the mobilize phase, reduce Phase 3 intensity by 50% that session. Persistent warm-up stiffness is an early symptom of developing tendinopathy.
  • Pain above 3 out of 10 during pogo hops: Stop Phase 3. Continue with the session using only walking and low-load calf work. Pain during plyometric priming indicates the tendon is not ready for reactive loading that day.
  • Localized swelling or warmth at the tendon mid-substance or insertion: Do not train through this. Swelling indicates an acute inflammatory response. Rest and consult a physiotherapist before returning to full loading.
  • A "crunching" or "creaking" sensation during ankle circles: Crepitus in the Achilles tendon sheath can indicate paratendinopathy. It does not necessarily mean you cannot train, but it warrants monitoring and a conversation with a sports medicine professional if it persists beyond a week.
  • Pain that improves dramatically mid-session but returns afterwards: This is the classic tendinopathy pattern. It means the tendon is managing load during activity through temperature and blood flow, but the underlying pathology is present. See our guide on preventing Achilles tears for the appropriate loading progression in this scenario.

Making This a Non-Negotiable Habit

Eight minutes is genuinely nothing. It is shorter than most athletes spend on their phone between arriving at the venue and starting activity. The barrier to implementation is not time ‚Äî it is habit. The most effective way to embed this sequence is to attach it to an existing pre-session behaviour: the moment you lace your shoes, you begin ankle circles. No conscious decision required.

Athletes who use this warm-up consistently report that it also functions as a daily check-in with their tendon health. When something feels different ‚Äî stiffer, more sensitive, asymmetric ‚Äî they catch it at Phase 1 rather than discovering it at the first sprint. That early detection, more than any single exercise, is what prevents minor tendon irritation from becoming a structural injury that sidelines you for months.
