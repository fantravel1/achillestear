6 Objective Criteria for Returning to Sport After Achilles Rupture

February 7, 2026 9 min read

Your surgeon cleared you at six months. Your physio said twelve months was the gold standard. Your training partner was back on the pitch at nine. Yet re-rupture rates remain stubbornly high — and the primary driver is not bad luck. It is a return to sport decision made on the basis of time elapsed rather than tissue readiness. These two things are not the same, and conflating them costs athletes their careers.

This article lays out six objective, measurable criteria drawn from current sports medicine literature. Until all six are met, the risk of re-rupture is significantly elevated — regardless of how good the tendon feels, how confident the athlete is, or how much their team needs them back on the field.

Why "Time Since Surgery" Is a Poor Proxy for Readiness

The instinct to use a time-based return is understandable. It is simple, predictable, and gives both clinician and athlete a target to work toward. The problem is that it does not reflect the biology of tendon healing. Collagen remodeling — the process by which disorganized scar tissue is gradually replaced by aligned, load-bearing type I collagen — takes 12 to 18 months regardless of whether surgery was performed or conservative management was chosen.

At the nine-month mark, which is when many athletes are cleared to return, the tendon may have recovered 70–80% of its structural integrity on imaging. But MRI signal normalization and functional capacity are not the same thing. A tendon that looks acceptable on scan is not necessarily capable of absorbing the eccentric loads involved in sprinting, jumping, or rapid direction change at full competitive intensity.

Large-scale retrospective data consistently show re-rupture rates of 10–12% in athletes who return based on time alone, compared to 2–4% when return is gated by objective functional criteria. That is a three-to-five-fold difference in risk — the kind of difference that justifies a rigorous, criteria-based approach even when it feels frustratingly slow.

Plyometric Clearance: The Prerequisite Before Criteria Testing

Before you can meaningfully test any of the six criteria below, the tendon must be capable of tolerating repeated spring-like loading. The clinical benchmark for this is the single-leg pogo hop: continuous, low-amplitude, rapid-rhythm hops on a single foot, performed pain-free for a minimum of three consecutive weeks.

If single-leg pogo hops cause pain, provoke tendon stiffness the following morning, or cannot be sustained for 30+ seconds without compensation, the tendon is not yet ready for hop testing. Attempting hop tests too early not only risks injury — it also produces artificially low scores that may lead to unnecessary delays later in the process. Get the plyometric clearance right, then move to formal criteria assessment.

Plyometric prerequisite: Perform single-leg pogo hops (continuous, rapid, low-amplitude) pain-free for 3+ consecutive weeks before beginning formal criteria testing. Tendon soreness rated above 3/10 on the VAS during or within 24 hours of hopping indicates the tendon is not yet ready.

The Traffic Light Framework

Before diving into the individual criteria, it helps to position yourself within a simple three-phase framework. This framework determines whether you are ready to test, ready to return, or somewhere in between.

Red — Do Not Test Yet

Morning stiffness present, pogo hops not tolerated, or fewer than 10 weeks post-plyometric introduction. Prioritize load management and tissue preparation.

Amber — Criteria Testing Phase

Plyometric clearance achieved, pogo hops tolerated 3+ weeks. Begin working through the 6 criteria systematically. Not yet cleared for unrestricted return.

Green — Graded Return

All 6 criteria met. Begin graduated return to sport protocol — starting with non-contact training, then contact practice, then competitive play.

The 6 Objective Return-to-Sport Criteria

Each criterion below is evidence-based, measurable, and has a specific numerical target. All six must be met before a graded return to unrestricted sport is appropriate. An athlete who meets five of six is not yet ready — partial clearance is not clearance.

01
Single-Leg Calf Raise Endurance
Target: ≥25 consecutive repetitions OR less than 10% deficit compared to the uninvolved leg

The single-leg heel raise to fatigue is the most practical and most widely validated functional test for Achilles tendon loading capacity. The athlete stands on one leg, with the knee straight, and rises fully onto the ball of the foot, then lowers under control. The test continues until form breaks down or the athlete cannot complete a full range of motion repetition. A score below 25 reps indicates the plantarflexors are not yet capable of sustaining the repetitive loading demands of running and jumping. The limb symmetry comparison (less than 10% deficit) is the more clinically meaningful threshold when the uninjured leg provides a reliable reference. See our guide on the single-leg calf raise for full technique notes and progressions.

02
Single-Leg Hop Test — Distance
Target: ≥90% Limb Symmetry Index (LSI) compared to uninvolved leg

The single-leg hop for distance measures explosive concentric and eccentric power through the entire lower limb, with a substantial contribution from plantarflexion. The athlete hops as far as possible on one leg and sticks the landing — both takeoff and landing must be on the same foot. Three trials are recorded; the best two are averaged. The Limb Symmetry Index is calculated as (injured leg ÷ uninvolved leg) × 100. Achieving 90% LSI indicates that propulsive force generation is approaching symmetry. An LSI below 85% should prompt further strengthening before re-testing. Find this test in our exercise library.

03
Triple Hop for Distance
Target: ≥90% Limb Symmetry Index (distance over 3 consecutive hops)

The triple hop extends the demands of the single-leg hop by requiring three consecutive hops on the same limb, with a controlled landing on the third. This test stresses repeated energy storage and release in the Achilles-calf complex and is more sensitive to deficits in reactive strength than the single-leg hop alone. An athlete who passes the single-leg hop but fails the triple hop often has adequate peak strength but insufficient tendon stiffness and elastic energy return — exactly the deficit that elevates re-rupture risk during repeated-sprint sport. The 90% LSI threshold applies equally here. If triple hop LSI is below 85%, graded plyometric loading over 3–4 weeks is typically sufficient to close the gap before re-testing.

04
Pain and Morning Stiffness Clearance
Target: No morning stiffness AND VAS pain ≤3/10 for 2+ consecutive weeks

Tendon pain during loading is a poor prognostic indicator for return to sport. The Visual Analogue Scale (VAS) provides a quick self-reported measure: the athlete rates tendon pain on a 0–10 scale during and within 24 hours of training. A score above 3/10 on the VAS at any point during two consecutive weeks indicates the tendon is still in a reactive or dysrepair state. Morning stiffness — the characteristic first-step stiffness upon waking that eases after 5–10 minutes of walking — is a reliable marker of tendon load accumulation. Persistent morning stiffness signals that the previous day's training exceeded the tendon's current adaptive capacity and that loading intensity must be reduced before criteria testing can be meaningful.

05
Isokinetic Plantarflexion Strength
Target: ≥90% peak torque compared to uninvolved side (tested at 60°/s and 120°/s)

Isokinetic dynamometry provides the most objective and reproducible measure of plantarflexion strength. The athlete pushes through a standardized range of motion against a calibrated resistance at a controlled angular velocity. Testing at 60 degrees per second captures slow-speed strength; 120 degrees per second introduces a velocity component closer to sport demands. Achieving 90% of the uninvolved side's peak torque at both speeds is the accepted threshold for strength symmetry. In clinical settings without access to a dynamometer, a validated alternative is the weighted single-leg heel raise: if the athlete can perform 8 reps with 30% of bodyweight added via a weight vest or loaded barbell on the injured side vs. the uninjured side without a deficit exceeding 10%, this approximates the 90% strength threshold. See the full recovery guide for loading progressions leading up to this benchmark.

06
Psychological Readiness — ACL-RSI Adapted
Target: Fear of re-injury score below 30% on the ACL-RSI adapted scale

Psychological readiness is the most undervalued and least frequently assessed criterion in Achilles rehabilitation. The ACL-Return to Sport after Injury (ACL-RSI) scale has been adapted for Achilles tendon rupture and measures three domains: emotions related to return to sport, confidence in performance, and fear of re-injury. A composite score below 30% on the fear subscale indicates that kinesiophobia — fear-avoidance of loading — is not likely to cause protective movement compensation that increases re-injury risk. Athletes who score above 30% on the fear subscale consistently underperform on functional hop tests and demonstrate altered gait mechanics even when physical criteria are met. Psychological readiness should be assessed formally using the questionnaire, not inferred from the athlete's verbal confidence.

Applying the Criteria: What Happens When You Score Below Threshold

The purpose of criteria-based return is not to create an obstacle course — it is to direct targeted training at the specific deficits that remain. Each criterion below threshold has a corresponding training response.

  • Calf raise endurance below 25 reps: Progress the single-leg heel raise protocol from flat surface to off-step, then add external load incrementally. Refer to our calf strength benchmarks guide for a structured 4-week protocol.
  • Hop test LSI below 90%: Introduce reactive plyometrics — single-leg bounding, box step-offs, and depth drops — before retesting in 3–4 weeks.
  • Persistent pain or morning stiffness: Reduce training volume by 30–40% and re-introduce load progressively over 2 weeks. Do not test criteria until two full pain-free weeks are achieved.
  • Isokinetic strength below 90%: Add progressive resistance to the heel raise, incorporate hip and knee chain loading to address full kinetic chain deficits, and retest at 4 weeks.
  • Psychological readiness score above 30%: Engage a sports psychologist for imagery, exposure therapy, and confidence-building strategies alongside physical rehabilitation.

The Bottom Line

Returning to sport after Achilles tendon rupture is not a single decision — it is a process. The six criteria outlined here replace the calendar as the arbiter of readiness. When an athlete meets all six, the biological and functional case for graded return is sound. When any criterion is unmet, continuing to load sport-specific demands is a gamble with re-rupture odds that no competitive season is worth.
