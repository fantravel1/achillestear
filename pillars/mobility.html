The Stretching Science

Dynamic vs. Static Stretching: When to Use Each

The wrong stretching at the wrong time harms performance. Master the timing and technique.

Dynamic Stretching (Before Activity)

Movement-based stretching. Gently moves the joint through its range of motion. Done before training or competition.

Ankle Circles: Stand on one leg, make slow circles with your other foot. 20 circles each direction, both feet. 1 minute total.
Leg Swings: Swing one leg forward and back 15 times, then side to side 15 times. Both legs. 2 minutes total.
Walking Leg Cradle: Walk forward, pull one knee up and across your body. Repeat with other leg. 20 meters.
Calf Raises While Walking: Walk on your toes for 20 meters. Activates the calf, opens the ankle.

Why before activity: Dynamic stretching increases heart rate, blood flow, and nervous system activation. Perfect for warming up.

Static Stretching (After Activity)

Hold stretches for 20â€“30 seconds. Done after training or competition when muscles are already warm.

Calf Stretch (Wall): Place hands on wall, step back with one leg (keep heel on ground), lean into the wall. Hold 30 seconds. Repeat other leg. 3 sets each leg.
Calf Stretch (Stair): Stand on a step, lower your heel below step level. Hold 30 seconds. Repeat. 3 sets each leg.
Soleus Stretch (Seated): Sit, cross one leg over the other, pull toes toward your shin. Hold 30 seconds. Repeat. 3 sets.
Plantarfascia Stretch: Stand, place ball of foot on a rolled towel, roll your foot over it. 1 minute total. Both feet.

Why after activity: Static stretching increases flexibility and range of motion. Does NOT improve performance if done before activity. Save it for post-workout.

The Daily Mobility Routine (5 Minutes)

Do this every single day, even rest days. Non-negotiable for ankle health.

Time allocation:

  • Ankle circles: 1 minute
  • Calf stretches: 2 minutes
  • Soleus stretch: 1 minute
  • Plantarfascia stretch: 1 minute

Best time: Evening or post-workout. Your muscles are warm.

Advanced Mobility

5 Ankle Mobility Drills

These drills build functional ankle mobility. Do 2â€“3x per week in addition to stretching.

Drill 1: Alphabet Writing

Use your foot to "write" the alphabet in the air. This moves your ankle through full range of motion.

Setup: Sit or stand. Extend one leg, toes pointed forward.
Movement: Imagine writing uppercase letters Aâ€“Z using just your foot (from the ankle). Big, exaggerated movements.
Duration: One full alphabet = 1 set. Do 3 sets per foot.

Drill 2: 4-Way Ankle Band Work

Using a resistance band to strengthen the ankle in all directions. Improves stability and mobility.

Plantarflexion (Point Toes): Band around ball of foot. Pull toes down 15 times. 3 sets.
Dorsiflexion (Lift Toes): Band around ball of foot. Lift toes up 15 times. 3 sets.
Inversion (Turn In): Band around ball of foot. Turn sole of foot inward 15 times. 3 sets.
Eversion (Turn Out): Band around ball of foot. Turn sole of foot outward 15 times. 3 sets.

Drill 3: Lunge and Reach

Combines hip and ankle mobility while building functional movement.

Step 1: Lunge forward with one leg, keeping back heel on ground.
Step 2: Reach up and across with opposite arm.
Step 3: Alternate legs. 10 lunges per leg. 3 sets.

Drill 4: Squats With Heel Emphasis

Squats build ankle dorsiflexion (the ability to lift your toes). Essential for safe movement.

Step 1: Perform bodyweight squats, emphasizing dorsiflexion (lifting your toes as you descend).
Step 2: Go as deep as comfortable while keeping heels on the ground.
Step 3: 15 squats, 3 sets.

Drill 5: Single-Leg Balance on Uneven Surface

Standing balance on an unstable surface (pillow, foam pad) forces your ankle to work harder, improving proprioception.

Step 1: Stand on one leg on a pillow or foam pad (unstable surface).
Step 2: Hold for 60 seconds. If you lose balance, reset and continue.
Step 3: 3 sets per leg.
Progression: Close your eyes halfway through. Remove visual feedback, forcing your ankle to stabilize alone.
