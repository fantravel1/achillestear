The Foundation Rule

The 10% Rule: How to Increase Training Load Safely

Never increase weekly volume (distance, reps, duration, intensity) more than 10% from one week to the next.

Why 10%?

Your Achilles adapts on a 2â€“4 week cycle. Collagen cross-linking â€” the biological process that builds strength â€” requires time. A 10% jump allows adaptation without overwhelming the tendon.

If you jump 20â€“30%, your Achilles can't keep up. Tendinopathy develops silently for 2â€“3 weeks, then you suffer a rupture during a normal workout.

What Counts as "Load"?

Don't just count miles or reps. Account for:

  • Volume: Total distance/reps per week
  • Intensity: Percentage of max effort (easy vs. tempo vs. sprint)
  • Frequency: How many times per week you train
  • Impact: Jump intensity and plyometric volume
  • Surface: Hard courts vs. grass vs. trail (surface changes increase load)

The 10% Rule in Practice

Example (Running):

  • Week 1: 30 miles total
  • Week 2: 33 miles (+10%)
  • Week 3: 36 miles (+10%)
  • Week 4: 30 miles (deload/recovery week)
  • Week 5: 33 miles (resume)

Example (Strength):

  • Week 1: 3 sets of 10 reps @ 100 lbs
  • Week 2: 3 sets of 12 reps @ 100 lbs (volume +20%)
  • Week 3: 3 sets of 12 reps @ 110 lbs (load +10%)
  • Week 4: Deload

Training Periodization: 16-Week Framework

Structure your training in cycles. This is how elite athletes prevent injuries while building peak performance.

Weeks 1â€“4: Base Building Phase

Goal: Build aerobic/strength foundation. Low intensity, moderate volume. Establish good habits and technique.

  1. Take a full recovery week (light activity, lots of mobility and sleep)
  2. Start a new 16-week cycle with slightly higher targets
  3. Shift to a maintenance phase (3â€“4 months of lower-intensity training)
