Running — Road Runner

Protect Your Achilles On Every Road Run.

Road running is the most accessible form of endurance sport — and one of the highest-volume Achilles loading activities available. The repetitive pavement contact without surface variation or adequate eccentric maintenance is the primary driver of tendinopathy in recreational runners.

When Achilles Problems Develop in Road Running

Four patterns drive Achilles tendinopathy in road runners. Each is preventable with the right training approach.

Pavement Accumulation

Concrete and asphalt return 95–100% of impact force directly to the runner. Unlike trail or grass surfaces, there is no shock absorption in the surface. Over 1000+ steps per kilometre, the cumulative Achilles load across a week of road running is enormous.

The Fix: Surface variation: mix road with trail, treadmill, or track at least 1–2 sessions per week. Rotate footwear.

The Marathon Taper Spike

Reducing mileage during taper while maintaining intensity creates a relative load spike — the tendons are less conditioned to high-intensity efforts by the time race day arrives. The race itself is often the highest single-day Achilles loading event.

The Fix: Maintain eccentric heel drops through the taper. Race simulation sessions 3 weeks out to prepare the tendon.
üìê

Cambered Road Loading

Running on cambered roads (sloped toward the gutter) places the outside foot in a persistently inverted position. One Achilles is loaded in a biomechanically disadvantaged position for the entire run, session after session.

The Fix: Alternate running directions on cambered roads. Bilateral eccentric training to correct asymmetric loading.
üèÅ

Race-Day Intensity

Racing harder than any training run creates a loading event the Achilles has never experienced. Adrenaline masks early warning signs. Most race-day Achilles ruptures occur in the second half of the race when fatigue compounds the load.

The Fix: Race simulation at goal pace 3 and 6 weeks before major races. Eccentric maintenance throughout the training block.
Pre-Training Protocol

Road Runner Achilles Activation (8 Minutes)

Complete before every road run. Never skip this on cold mornings or after rest days.

1

Ankle Circles (2 min)

10 slow circles each direction per foot. Non-negotiable on cold mornings — tendon stiffness is highest in the first 15 minutes.

2

Calf Pumps (2 min)

3×30 moderate-pace toe raises. Increases Achilles blood flow before pavement loading.

3

Easy Walk-to-Jog Transition (2 min)

2 min brisk walking, then a 1 min 6:30/km jog. Never start road running at training pace. Always build.

4

Gradual Pace Build (1 min)

2×100 m gradual acceleration from easy to target pace. Tests the Achilles before committing to training pace.

5

Single-Leg Balance Check (1 min)

30 s per leg. If balance feels off or the Achilles feels stiff after circles, extend warm-up before running.

Training Protocol

Road Runner Strength Block (12 Weeks)

3 sessions per week, 20–25 minutes. Progressive overload every 2 weeks.

Weeks 1–4: Tendon Capacity Base

Eccentric Heel Drops — 3×15 daily, straight-leg on a step. Lower over 4 seconds on one foot. The non-negotiable foundation.
Isometric Calf Hold — 3×45 s, 3×/week. Rise on toes, hold at top. Builds tendon stiffness for high-speed demands.
Seated Calf Raises — 3×20, 3×/week. Knee bent 90°. Targets soleus — often neglected and highly protective.
Single-Leg Balance — 3×30 s per leg. Builds proprioception critical for all track and field movements.

Weeks 5–8: Power Development

Weighted Eccentric Heel Drops — Add 10 lb in a backpack. 3×12 daily. Accelerated tendon adaptation.
Heavy Slow Resistance Calf Raises — 3×12 at 70–80% effort, 3×/week. Maximum collagen-synthesis stimulus.
Pogo Hops — 3×30 s, 2×/week. Rapid ankle-only hops. Trains the stretch-shortening cycle.
Lateral Bounds — 3×8 per side, 2×/week. Explosive side-to-side loading.

Weeks 9–12: Sport-Specific Power

Single-Leg Hops for Distance — 3×5 per leg, 2×/week. Maximise push-off distance, control landing.
Plyometric Box Jumps — 3×6, 2×/week. Step down — don’t jump down. Builds explosion safely.
Sprint Acceleration Drills — 3×20 m at 85% speed, 2×/week. Progressive speed work for tendon readiness.
Eccentric Heel Drops (Maintenance) — 3×15, 3×/week. Never stop. In-season tendon maintenance.
