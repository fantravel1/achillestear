Protect Your Achilles Across Every Mile.

Ultra running creates loading demands that no other sport approaches — 50 to 200+ miles over 24–48 hours, with night running, sleep deprivation, cumulative terrain load, and pack weight all compounding the Achilles risk. Preparation is everything.

Ultra Runner Risk

When Achilles Problems Develop in Ultra Running

Ultra running creates Achilles risks that are unique in their scale and duration. Four key scenarios define the ultra runner’s vulnerability.

Night Running Mechanics Degradation

After 16–20 hours of racing, neuromuscular fatigue causes running mechanics to break down. Landing mechanics become heel-heavy, ankle control deteriorates, and the Achilles absorbs forces it is not positioned to handle. The majority of Achilles ruptures in 100-mile races occur at night.

The Fix: Fatigue-resistant Achilles conditioning: train the eccentric capacity to maintain form under fatigue. Include night running in long training runs.

Cumulative Descent Load

Ultra events with 5000–10 000+ m of elevation gain create matching descent volumes. Downhill running places the Achilles in a maximally lengthened position under eccentric load with every stride. Across hours of descent, this loading far exceeds anything in shorter-distance events.

The Fix: Progressive descent training: include 20–30% downhill in long runs. Eccentric drops on declined surfaces 2×/week.
üéí

Pack Weight Addition

Race vests and pack requirements add 3–10 kg to every step. Over 50–100 miles, each additional kilogram of carried weight adds substantial cumulative Achilles load. Athletes who train without a pack and race with one are at elevated risk.

The Fix: Loaded training protocol: train with race-weight pack in the final 8 weeks before the event.
üí§

Sleep Deprivation Tissue Recovery

Tendon collagen repair depends on deep sleep. Consecutive nights without adequate sleep during training blocks, or the sleep deprivation inherent in 24–48 hour races, dramatically impairs tendon recovery between loading events.

The Fix: Strategic race sleep protocol: plan sleep windows in 100-mile races. Prioritise sleep in the week before the event. Monitor training load during high-stress life periods.
Pre-Training Protocol

Ultra Runner Achilles Activation (10 Minutes)

Complete at the start of every long training run and at race start. Critical on cold mornings and after sleep windows.

1

Extended Ankle Mobility (3 min)

Circles, alphabet, slow inversion and eversion. Ultra runners need more time in this phase — tendon stiffness accumulates over multi-day events.

2

Progressive Walk-to-Jog (3 min)

5 min brisk walking then 3 min easy jog. Never start at training pace. In ultra events, always walk the first kilometre after sleep or rest stops.

3

Calf Pumps (2 min)

3×30 calf pumps. Maintains Achilles blood flow throughout extended events — repeat every 4–6 hours during races.

4

Single-Leg Balance (1 min)

30 s per leg. After sleep windows or extended rest in a race, always include this before returning to running pace.

5

Downhill Simulation (1 min)

5 slow controlled downhill strides before any descent section. Prepares the eccentric capacity for descent loading.

Training Protocol

Ultra Runner Strength Block (12 Weeks)

3 sessions per week, 20–25 minutes. Progressive overload every 2 weeks.

Weeks 1–4: Tendon Capacity Base

Eccentric Heel Drops — 3×15 daily, straight-leg on a step. Lower over 4 seconds on one foot. The non-negotiable foundation.
Isometric Calf Hold — 3×45 s, 3×/week. Rise on toes, hold at top. Builds tendon stiffness for high-speed demands.
Seated Calf Raises — 3×20, 3×/week. Knee bent 90°. Targets soleus — often neglected and highly protective.
Single-Leg Balance — 3×30 s per leg. Builds proprioception critical for all track and field movements.

Weeks 5–8: Power Development

Weighted Eccentric Heel Drops — Add 10 lb in a backpack. 3×12 daily. Accelerated tendon adaptation.
Heavy Slow Resistance Calf Raises — 3×12 at 70–80% effort, 3×/week. Maximum collagen-synthesis stimulus.
Pogo Hops — 3×30 s, 2×/week. Rapid ankle-only hops. Trains the stretch-shortening cycle.
Lateral Bounds — 3×8 per side, 2×/week. Explosive side-to-side loading.

Weeks 9–12: Sport-Specific Power

Single-Leg Hops for Distance — 3×5 per leg, 2×/week. Maximise push-off distance, control landing.
Plyometric Box Jumps — 3×6, 2×/week. Step down — don’t jump down. Builds explosion safely.
Sprint Acceleration Drills — 3×20 m at 85% speed, 2×/week. Progressive speed work for tendon readiness.
Eccentric Heel Drops (Maintenance) — 3×15, 3×/week. Never stop. In-season tendon maintenance.
